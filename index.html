Who we are

Built on
shared ground.

Common Ground exists because cities are too important to be misunderstood.

We started with a simple frustration: people make life-defining decisions about where to live, work, and invest, based on gut feeling, incomplete data, or maps that only show geography, not livability.

Common Ground was built to change that. By weaving together open data on transit, green space, civic infrastructure, and accessibility, we produce a layered, honest portrait of what neighborhoods are actually like to inhabit.

The platform is designed to be understood by anyone, not just those with technical backgrounds or institutional access. Urban intelligence should be a public good.

  • 01
    Transparency first
    Every data source is documented. Every methodology is open. We don't hide how we reach our conclusions.
  • 02
    Designed for everyone
    From a student writing a geography essay to a city planner allocating bus routes — the same platform serves both, equally.
  • 03
    Data with context
    Numbers without meaning are noise. We layer data with historical context, equity analysis, and local nuance.
  • 04
    Useful, not decorative
    We build tools people actually act on — not dashboards that look impressive in a deck and collect dust after.

The person behind it
Prathamesh Parkhi
High School Junior — Passion Project, 2026

Common Ground began as my high school passion project, the kind that starts with a question you can't stop thinking about. For me, that question was simple: why is it so hard to actually understand a neighborhood?

What started as a personal research exercise grew into a full platform. I built Common Ground with the conviction that urban data, the kind that shapes where people live, how they move, and what resources they access, should be legible to anyone, not just those with a GIS degree or a city council budget. This is that attempt.

Cities are
made of
people, not
polygons.

The most important things about a neighborhood can't be captured in a satellite image, the frequency of the bus you take to work, the distance to the nearest park, whether your street has a library nearby.

I believe that access to this kind of deep, layered urban knowledge shouldn't be the exclusive province of consultants and institutions. It belongs to residents, researchers, students, and planners alike.

That's what common ground means: the shared understanding of cities that makes better decisions possible.

What we're building toward

Clear goals.
Real impact.

Common Ground is structured around six interlocking objectives — each one a commitment to how we build, what we prioritize, and who we serve.

01

Democratize urban data access

Make high-quality neighborhood intelligence available to anyone with an internet connection — free of paywalls, free of jargon, and free of institutional gatekeeping. Urban data belongs to urban residents.

Access
02

Surface inequality in infrastructure

Use data to make visible what's often invisible ,the systematic underinvestment in certain neighborhoods, the transit deserts, the green space gaps. Name the disparities so they can be addressed.

Equity
03

Support evidence-based planning

Give city planners, researchers, and policy teams a reliable, integrated data layer, so decisions about where to build, invest, and improve are grounded in reality, not assumption.

Planning
04

Enable informed resident decisions

Help families, students, and individuals understand neighborhoods before they move, study, or advocate, giving them the same depth of insight once reserved for corporate site-selection consultants.

Residents
05

Build open, reproducible methodology

Every score, index, and insight we produce is fully documented and openly replicable. We publish our methods so researchers can extend, challenge, and improve what we've built.

Openness
06

Expand coverage across cities

Grow from our founding network to comprehensive coverage globally, prioritizing cities where data gaps are largest and the need for urban intelligence is most acute.
